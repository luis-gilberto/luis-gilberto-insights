If there's one thing I've learned in this season of reinvention, it's that every setback comes with a hidden syllabus. It's a curriculum you don't ask for, but one that teaches you more than any title ever could… if you're willing to lean in and listen.

My time at Microsoft shaped me in countless ways: strategically, creatively, and professionally. But it was what came after that taught me the most about myself.

After years of contributing to high-impact campaigns and storytelling at scale, I found myself facing an unexpected career pivot. Not by choice—but perhaps by design. This wasn't just time off. It was time in. Time to get quiet, curious, and intentional. I broke the productivity spell, reconnected with my creative rhythm, and started asking questions about what success could look like on my own terms.

The AI Speed Paradox

Right before my transition, a mantra began echoing across the organization: "Move at the speed of AI." Each time I heard it, something in me tightened; because the truth is, we're not machines. We're human. And sometimes the most radical move you can make is to slow down.

I didn't realize how addicted I'd become to being productive until I stopped. The first few weeks were jarring. I didn't miss the meetings or inbox chaos. I missed the validation. I had no sprint to run, no one to impress, and no reason to polish executive updates or PowerPoint decks at 11:58 PM. Without that drumbeat of urgency, I felt lost, floaty, and untethered.

That was the withdrawal.

We are not machines. We're not built for infinite acceleration. And pretending we are, while catchy, is the kind of logic that burns out great people under the banner of innovation.

The Journey: Output vs. Meaningful Impact

So, I slowed down. I gave myself permission to stop optimizing every second. I learned things that had no deliverables. I sat with discomfort instead of trying to outrun it. I took silent walks. Cooked meals for no reason. Measured my days not by output, but by alignment.

It was uncomfortable. And exactly what I needed.

A Website, a Blank Page, and the Return of Curiosity

Once the noise quieted, curiosity came back. Real curiosity. Not the performative kind you add to LinkedIn bios. The kind that keeps you up late, not from anxiety, but from excitement.

That's how my portfolio site was born. No agency. No team. Just me, a blank Framer canvas, some AI-powered sidekicks, and one big question: what would it look like to design something that feels like me?

It turned out to be more than a website. It became a playground for everything I'd been learning and unlearning. Branding, typography, UX, storytelling—I pieced it all together intuitively, imperfectly, and proudly at luis-gilberto.com.

Somewhere between debugging CSS and choosing font weights, I realized I wasn't just building a portfolio. I was rebuilding myself.

Rest Isn't the End. Sometimes It's the Beginning.

If you're in a season of uncertainty; feeling underestimated, unmoored, or just plain tired: you're not broken. You may simply be between versions. And what's emerging might surprise you.
